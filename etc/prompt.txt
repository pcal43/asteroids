This project contains a skeleton for a PICO-8 game. It includes a top-level controller, a title screen, and a game screen. The project establishes conventions for object-oriented-style Lua using coarse-grained objects (e.g., screens with update() and draw() methods).

Extend this skeleton to implement a game inspired by Atari Asteroids using purely geometric rendering. All graphics must be drawn using line() and circ()/circfill(). Do not use sprites.

All gameplay logic should live in GameScreen.lua. Follow the object-oriented conventions already present. Define objects (Lua tables with methods) such as Ship, Asteroid, Bullet, and UFO. Each object should implement update(dt) and draw(). Maintain collections for asteroids, bullets, enemy_bullets, and ufos. Use a consistent delta time (dt, in seconds) for all movement and timing.

The player controls a spaceship represented by an isosceles triangle. The triangle should have a 30-degree nose angle and a height of 12 pixels, drawn in white using line(). The ship starts at the center of the screen (64,64), facing upward, with zero velocity.

Controls: Left rotates counterclockwise and Right rotates clockwise at 180 degrees per second, continuously while held. Pressing the X button applies thrust in the ship’s facing direction with acceleration of 10 px/sec². When not thrusting, apply velocity damping at 1 px/sec² until velocity reaches zero. Cap the maximum ship velocity at 20 px/sec. While thrusting, draw a red equilateral triangle behind the ship, pointing opposite the ship’s direction.

Pressing the O button fires a bullet from the ship’s nose in the current facing direction. Bullets travel at 20 px/sec and disappear after traveling 64 pixels. Bullets are 2 pixels in size and orange in color. Add a small firing cooldown of approximately 0.2 seconds.

At the start of the game, spawn 10 large asteroids at random positions, ensuring none are within 20 pixels of the player. Asteroids move in random directions at speeds between 5 and 10 px/sec.

There are three asteroid sizes: large (16px diameter), medium (12px), and small (8px). Asteroids are drawn as outlined circles using circ(). When a large asteroid is hit, it splits into two medium asteroids. Medium asteroids split into two small asteroids. Small asteroids are destroyed when hit. New asteroids inherit the parent position and move in random directions.

Use simple circular collision detection for all interactions. Player bullets destroy asteroids. The player ship is destroyed on collision with an asteroid. Player bullets destroy UFOs.

The game world wraps on all edges (torus topology). If an object moves past one edge of the 128x128 screen, it should appear on the opposite side. This applies to the ship, asteroids, bullets, and UFOs.

The player starts with 3 lives. When the ship is destroyed, respawn it at the center with zero velocity and grant temporary invulnerability (approximately 2 seconds). Display remaining lives as small upward-pointing isosceles triangles (8px) in the upper-left corner.

The player earns points: 10 for large asteroids, 20 for medium, 30 for small, and 100 for destroying a UFO. Display the score in the upper-right corner.

When all asteroids are destroyed, wait 3 seconds, then spawn a new wave of 10 large asteroids following the same rules.

At random intervals between 30 and 60 seconds, spawn a UFO. It travels horizontally across the screen (left-to-right or right-to-left) at approximately 15 px/sec. The UFO periodically fires 2px green bullets in random directions (about once every 1–2 seconds). UFO bullets can destroy both the player and asteroids, but do not award points for asteroid destruction. If the player’s bullet hits the UFO, it is destroyed and awards 100 points. If the UFO collides with the player, both are destroyed.

Ensure clean separation of update logic and rendering logic. The final implementation should be complete, readable, and idiomatic PICO-8 Lua, with no placeholder code.

Optional enhancements if space allows: simple particle effects, screen shake, sound effects using sfx(), and increasing difficulty across waves.
